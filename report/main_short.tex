\begin{abstract}[]
\addchaptertocentry{\abstractname}
Cognitive performance post Acquired Brain Injury (ABI) has been identified as one of the greatest predictors of long term functional outcome. However, the patients who need intensive rehabilitation are usually the ones who will be unable to complete a full neuropsychological battery. Therefore, missingness is informative in this context. Fatigue, confusion, motor limitations, test tolerance, and examiner judgment can limit the testing completed. As a result, retaining healthier assessments will favorably bias the selection of patients included in analyses and exclude the most impaired patients. While broad diagnostic categories and injury severity bands provide some way to group patients based upon similar characteristics, they do not capture the necessary variability required to create personalized rehabilitation plans.



This dissertation addresses these concerns through using a combination of missing-data imputation and unsupervised clustering. Complete assessments are retained and cognitive structures are inferred directly from test data. The dependency of the cognitive structures created by the selected imputation strategy is explicitly assessed.



Data were collected from 17{,}406 neuropsychological evaluations performed on 7{,}285 patients with ABI. Three cognitive-severity bands were identified: Above Average (38.6\%, $n = 6{,}725$); Near Normal (36.4\%, $n = 6{,}328$); and Global Impairment (25\%, $n = 4{,}353$). The main difference between these bands relates to the general level of cognition instead of profile shape. All six cognitive domains vary together, and one severity factor explains 56\% of their common variance. Hypothesis 1 was therefore partially supported: reproducible severity bands were recovered, but the evidence did not support sharply discrete phenotypes. Within this domain-aggregated density-clustering framework, there is no evidence to suggest an attention-specific dissociation exists.



The cognitive-severity assignments were internally consistent across imputation strategies under the assumed missing-data framework. Addressing Hypothesis 2, all ten imputation strategies met the pre-specified silhouette and noise criteria, although per-cluster bootstrap matching was only moderate and cluster count varied across methods; in the shared-pipeline analysis the average adjusted Rand index (ARI) between imputation strategies was 0.71, and the primary multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE) strategy had a full-coverage silhouette of 0.526 after noise reassignment. Supporting Hypothesis 3, tier assignment was associated with clinical unit (Cramér's $V=0.16$), though a patient-grouped multinomial sensitivity analysis did not show a clear improvement in out-of-sample unit classification once tier was added to the broad diagnostic grouping. Supporting Hypothesis 4, domain-level aggregation reduced redundancy and improved numerical conditioning over variable-level clustering, decreasing mean variance inflation factor (VIF) from 2.8 to 1.9 and condition number from 62 to 12, while cluster separation changed only marginally. Taken together, these findings support the use of the pipeline for severity-based triage as an initial step in personalized rehabilitation planning.



CogDash is a proof-of-concept interactive dashboard developed to demonstrate the pipeline and provide severity tier assignments with consensus confidence for research purposes. The code, notebooks and dashboard are accessible at \url{https://github.com/zkunos/thesis_project}.
\end{abstract}
